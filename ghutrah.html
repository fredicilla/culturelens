Eid Morning — Riyadh, Najd

You are getting dressed for Eid. Your grandfather stands behind you, watching every movement. Before you can tie your Ghutrah, he steps forward — and starts again from the beginning.

"How you wear this," he says, "tells the world who your family is."

The Thobe — الثوب

The Thobe comes first. In Najd, it is always white — long, clean, and pressed. The length must reach exactly the ankle. Too short means carelessness. Too long means arrogance.

The whiteness is intentional — it signals purity and readiness to receive guests.

2
The Ghutrah — الغترة

The Ghutrah is the white headscarf of Najd. Unlike the red-checkered Shumagh common in colder regions, the pure white Ghutrah signals you are from the heartland.

White Ghutrah = Najd & Riyadh region
Red Shumagh = Northern regions, colder climates
Tap to continue ▾
3
The Iqal — العقال

The Iqal — the black doubled cord — holds the Ghutrah in place. But its origins are far older than fashion. Bedouin tribesmen used the same rope to tether their camels at night, then wrap it on their heads by day.

Its thickness and weave once identified your tribe before you spoke a single word.
